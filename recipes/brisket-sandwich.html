Brisket Sandwich (I hope you like BBQ!)

The brisket sandwich is a great choice for meatlovers and goes great with so many sauces!

The brisket sandwich was first created in 1950 after the Second World War. Its creators had hoped to make a dish so irresistible that anyone who tried it would immediately give up any ambitions or hopes to conquer the world or do bad to others. In other words, the brisket sandwich became the first technology ever created with the sole intention of bringing about world peace.

As the brisket sandwich began making its way around the world, people were shocked, not only by its immaculate taste, but also for its incredibly beneficial psychological effects. Word quickly spread about its incredbly properties and soon everybody wanted to try a bite of this exquisite dish. As the brisket sandwich and its followers began to grow around the world, so did its enemies and the people who wanted to see its destruction. Eventually, two new superpowers arose with one side supporting the brisket sandwich and one seeking to destroy it. As nuclear war seemed to be the inevitable end, both sides struck a truce which allowed the brisket sandwich to retain its tasty properties but would have to forgo its tranquil psychological effects.

Ingredients

  • 8 pounds of Chef's Line© Slow Smoked Beer Brisket
  • Cross Valley Farms© Superfood Slaw
  • 1 cup mayonnaise
  • Rykoff Sexton© Dark Ale Mustard
  • Sriracha Pepper Sauce
  • Chef's Line© Bianco Buns

Steps

  1. Toast Bianco Buns, set to side. Prepare dressing for slaw by combining mayonnaise, mustard and Siracha. Add to slaw mixture.
  2. Reheat beer brisket in boil-in pouch. Slice entire brisket, incorporate both lean and marbled pieces for sandwiches. Reserve some of the cooking liquid to keep brisket moist. Keep hot prior to assembly.
  3. Assemble sandwich with 4 ounce sliced brisket topped with 2 ounces of spicy slaw on a toasted Bianco Bun.
  4. Serve with a side of potato salad, Metro DeliÂ® Chips or house pickles. Yield: 24 each, 6 ounce filling.
